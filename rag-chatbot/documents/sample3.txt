Viyom: A Transparent Blockchain-based Donation Fund Allocation
Platform
Sanika Gawande1, Pradnya Shinde1, Jay Kadam1, Kartika Handal1, and Bhavesh Jadhav1
Department of Computer Engineering, International Institute of Information Technology, Pune, India
sanikagawande7@gmail.com
Abstract. Philanthropic organizations across the globe continue to grapple with sustaining donor confidence
and demonstrating operational accountability. Conventional centralized donation infrastructures are burdened
by poor traceability, inadequate oversight mechanisms, and high administrative costs, all of which collectively
erode donor trust over time. Contemporary breakthroughs in distributed ledger technology present a compelling
pathway forward through decentralized architectures, tamper-resistant record-keeping, and smart
contract-driven automation. This paper introduces Viyom, a blockchain-powered platform for transparent
donation management and proportional fund disbursement, engineered to deliver secure, auditable, and
operationally efficient contribution handling. The system supports live fund monitoring, rule-based allocation
logic, and cryptographically verifiable transaction records that meaningfully strengthen accountability. A
comparative study of incumbent platforms and the proposed system is conducted to surface key architectural
patterns, security requirements, and engineering trade-offs. Findings confirm that distributed ledger integration
yields substantial gains in transparency and traceability, while acknowledging persistent obstacles around
throughput scalability, jurisdictional compliance, and mass-market adoption.
Keywords: Blockchain · Donation System · Smart Contracts · Fund Allocation · Transparency

1 Introduction
The worldwide philanthropic sector channels trillions of dollars annually, yet a profound opacity persists regarding
how individual contributions ultimately translate into measurable social outcomes [3, 6, 15]. Empirical data
suggests that roughly 70 percent of individual donors retain little to no visibility into how their donated funds are
apportioned or utilized, and possess no independent means of verification. This structural information gap
generates a substantial trust deficit that hampers long-term donor engagement [6, 15]. Traditional charitable
platforms depend heavily on centralized governing bodies—including nonprofit organizations and intermediary
aggregators—whose architectures introduce risks such as administrative mismanagement, retrospective auditing
failures, and susceptibility to fund misappropriation [9, 10]. Multiple well-documented cases illustrate how
concentrated control over financial flows can inflate overhead expenditures and dilute the impact experienced by
intended beneficiaries, ultimately undermining institutional credibility [6, 15].
Beyond financial opacity, centralized models restrict the degree of donor participation, attract avoidable
intermediary charges, and cannot deliver live insight into how contributions are being deployed [3, 5]. The
emergence of blockchain-based distributed ledger technology over the past decade introduces practical remedies to
these chronic structural deficiencies [1, 7, 8]. Core attributes of blockchain—namely decentralization,
cryptographic integrity assurance, consensus-enforced immutability, and open transaction transparency—directly
mitigate the trust and accountability shortcomings inherent in conventional philanthropic platforms [2, 5, 14].
Smart contract capabilities extend these benefits further by enabling autonomous donation processing,
rule-governed fund disbursement, and independently verifiable audit trails without any dependence on centralized
intermediaries [2, 11].
A growing body of scholarship demonstrates that blockchain-enabled philanthropic systems can meaningfully
elevate transparency, end-to-end traceability, and donor confidence—particularly in high-urgency contexts such as
disaster relief and large-scale humanitarian aid delivery [5, 6]. Notwithstanding these advantages, deployed
implementations continue to encounter difficulties related to transaction throughput, cost efficiency, regulatory
ambiguity, and accessibility barriers for users lacking technical expertise [8, 10, 13]. This paper synthesizes

contemporary academic findings and applied research on distributed ledger-based donation platforms, examining
Viyom as a representative case study designed to address outstanding gaps via cost-effective blockchain
infrastructure, automated allocation mechanisms, and closed-loop donor feedback channels [2, 5, 15]. By
systematically cataloguing existing systems and pinpointing unresolved research questions, this work advances
ongoing efforts to build transparent, scalable, and trustworthy digital philanthropy ecosystems.
Section 2 surveys relevant prior work and identifies limitations in existing donation systems. Section 3 details the
proposed Viyom platform and its operational workflow. Section 4 presents the mathematical model and system
formulation. Experimental observations and analysis are discussed in Section 5, followed by conclusions and
future research directions in Section 6.

2 Related Work
The literature in this domain has evolved through three broad developmental phases: analysis and limitations of
existing donation platforms, a survey of prior academic contributions, and identification of capability gaps that
current systems fail to address.
2.1 Existing Donation Platforms: Analysis and Limitations
A range of centralized web-based donation platforms are currently operational for charitable giving; however,
each exhibits significant shortcomings with respect to transparency and independent verification. GiveIndia, one
of the country's most prominent charitable aggregators, facilitates donor contributions to accredited nonprofit
bodies but operates on centralized database infrastructure that lacks any blockchain-backed verification layer [3,
6]. Consequently, donors are entirely dependent on organizational self-reporting for fund allocation information,
while auditing remains periodic rather than real-time [10, 15]. Milaap leverages personal narrative-driven
crowdfunding campaigns but remains rooted in a Web 2.0 centralized paradigm, providing minimal transaction
traceability and no cryptographic evidence of fund utilization [6, 15]. Ketto similarly supports medical and social
fundraising, yet suffers from centralized governance, platform fee structures, and weak accountability safeguards
that reduce net beneficiary impact [3, 6]. Conventional donation management systems employed by religious
institutions and community bodies frequently rely on manual ledger-keeping, paper-based receipts, and
retrospective reconciliation cycles [15]. These manual processes are susceptible to human error, delayed
reconciliations, and restricted donor-side visibility, collectively weakening both trust and operational efficiency [6,
10]. Such shortcomings underscore the pressing requirement for tamper-proof, real-time, and independently
verifiable donation tracking mechanisms.
2.2 Academic Research: Summary of Prior Research Papers
He et al. proposed a privacy-preserving charitable donation framework on the Ethereum network combined with
searchable encryption, allowing donors to query encrypted contribution records [2]. While this approach
successfully achieves immutability and enhanced privacy, the computational overhead introduced by encryption
constrains practical scalability, and manual intervention remains necessary for fund disbursement [2, 8]. Nairi et
al. explored smart blockchain networks for contribution tracking within Web 3.0 paradigms, highlighting
automation potential via smart contracts while concurrently acknowledging barriers posed by elevated transaction
fees and implementation complexity [4, 8]. Their investigation, however, remained largely conceptual and lacked
production-scale empirical validation [4, 10]. Mariyam et al. designed a blockchain-oriented transparency
framework for charitable transactions leveraging a public-permissioned Ethereum network [3]. Although technical
feasibility was established, the study surfaced material challenges around regulatory ambiguity and throughput
limitations when accommodating large concurrent donor volumes [3, 10]. Sarumathi et al. presented the
Blockchain Enabled Charity Process (BECP) framework with a specific focus on disaster response scenarios,
demonstrating blockchain's operational resilience when conventional financial infrastructure becomes unavailable
[5]. Smart contract-driven real-time fund distribution was successfully demonstrated, though scalability and
cross-system interoperability gaps persisted [5, 14].
2.3 Gap Identified: What Current Systems Fail to Provide

A consistent review of existing platforms reveals that none currently delivers: (1) live fund traceability, as no
system supports per-minute monitoring of donation flows to specific beneficiaries [3, 5, 15]; (2) cryptographic
auditability, since organizations cannot furnish mathematical proof of the immutability of their transaction history
[2, 7, 10]; (3) autonomous fund allocation, given that manual approval workflows introduce latency and subjective
decision-making [4, 5]; (4) cost-effective micropayment support, because elevated gas fees render small-value
donations economically unviable [8, 14]; (5) decentralized claim verification, since donors currently cannot
independently validate organizational assertions without institutional mediation [6, 15]; and (6) integration with
mainstream financial rails, as most blockchain-based solutions presuppose cryptocurrency familiarity that the
broader donor population does not possess [9, 13].

3 Methodology
3.1 Overview of the Proposed Approach
The proposed blockchain-enabled donation platform implements a comprehensive end-to-end workflow that
guarantees transparency, proportional fund allocation, and immutable auditability. The operational sequence
proceeds as follows:
1. Donor Registration and Authentication: Contributors establish accounts with email-based identity
verification and JSON Web Token (JWT) authentication.
2. Cause Selection: Donors nominate a specific charitable cause, spanning Gaushala, Education, Healthcare,
or Animal Welfare.
3. Donation Initiation: The contributor specifies the monetary value of their intended contribution for the
designated cause.
4. Payment Processing: The Razorpay payment gateway handles secure transaction settlement via UPI, debit
or credit cards, and digital wallets.
5. Backend Recording: The Spring Boot API persists donation metadata—including donor identifier, cause
identifier, timestamp, transaction reference, amount, and processing status—within a MySQL datastore.
6. Smart Contract Execution: A Solidity-based smart contract deployed on the Polygon blockchain is
invoked, producing a cryptographically immutable transaction hash.
7. Cause Pool Credit: The contributed sum is allocated to the appropriate cause pool and associated with the
donor's portfolio record.
8. Expenditure Recording: Platform administrators log disbursements from designated cause pools as they
occur.
9. Proportional Deduction Calculation: The system derives each donor's relative share of an expenditure
and applies the corresponding deduction to their individual balance.
10. Blockchain Anchoring: All expenditure entries and individual proportional deductions are permanently
committed to the blockchain ledger.
11. Notification and Dashboard Update: Donors are notified of relevant activity, and their portfolio
dashboards are refreshed to reflect updated balances.
3.2 Technology Stack
The implementation draws on a modern and complementary suite of web, server-side, and blockchain
technologies:

– Database: MySQL 8+ with ACID-compliant transaction guarantees
– Blockchain Layer: Polygon (Mumbai Testnet / Mainnet), Solidity smart contracts, Web3.js integration
– Payment Gateway: Razorpay API
– Authentication: JWT-based role-level access control

3.3 Blockchain Layer Architecture
The distributed ledger layer comprises a set of modular, purpose-specific smart contracts:
– Master Contract: Governs overarching platform parameters, manages cause pool instantiation, and
enforces administrator privileges.
– Cause Pool Contracts: Discrete contracts scoped to each individual charitable cause.
– Proportional Deduction Contract: Encodes and executes the proportional allocation algorithm.
– Donor Portfolio Contract: Maintains a persistent record of each donor's contributions, deductions, and
net balances.
– Expenditure Recording Contract: Archives disbursement metadata and associated on-chain
transaction references.
3.4 Security Framework
Platform security is reinforced through a layered combination of cryptographic protocols and architectural
controls:
– Cryptographic Hashing: SHA-256 guarantees the integrity of donation and expenditure records.
– Smart Contract Authorization: Expenditure approvals are restricted exclusively to permissioned
administrator accounts.
– JWT Expiration Policies: Time-bounded authentication tokens mitigate replay attack risks.
– Webhook Signature Verification: Validates payment gateway callbacks to prevent fraudulent
confirmation injections.
– Immutable Ledger Storage: Blockchain consensus mechanisms preclude retrospective data
modification.
– Secure Key Management: Cryptographic private keys are stored within Hardware Security Modules
(HSMs).
– Role-Based Access Control: Granular permission tiers appropriately segregate donor and
administrative system access.

3.5 Real-Time Tracking and Notification Mechanism
Upon successful transaction completion, donors immediately receive the corresponding blockchain transaction
hash for independent verification. When platform administrators record an expenditure, automated notifications
are dispatched to all relevant donors containing:
– A description of the expenditure and the designated beneficiary
– The aggregate amount disbursed from the relevant cause pool
– The donor's individually computed proportional deduction
– Their updated residual contribution balance
– The on-chain transaction hash enabling independent ledger verification
Donors retain persistent access to their complete contribution portfolio, inclusive of blockchain-backed
verification references, at any time through the donor dashboard.

4.1 Assumptions
The model is grounded in the following operational assumptions:
– Every donation undergoes immediate on-chain validation at the time of submission.
– Transaction validation latency is treated as a fixed constant for modelling purposes.
– Each fund allocation event is unique and individually subject to cryptographic verification.
– Financial reporting is generated at configurable periodic intervals encompassing daily, weekly, monthly,
and annual granularities.

5 Discussion
The development of the Viyom Donation Platform illustrates how the deliberate integration of a centralized
relational database with a decentralized blockchain layer can materially advance transparency and organizational
accountability within donation-based ecosystems. The system was evaluated across four principal functional
dimensions: donation flow processing, fund allocation logic, data consistency guarantees, and transparency
assurance mechanisms.

Regarding backend performance, the MySQL-powered relational layer demonstrated reliable handling of
structured data pertaining to donors, charitable sectors, cause pools, and allocation records. A normalized schema
design curtailed data redundancy and preserved transactional integrity throughout system operations. During
controlled partial testing, queries targeting donation aggregation and allocation tracking exhibited stable
performance under moderate concurrent loads, suggesting the database architecture is well-suited for
small-to-medium deployment contexts. Selective indexing on high-frequency query attributes—most notably
sector identifier and donation processing status—produced measurable gains in retrieval speed, especially for
dashboard analytics workloads.
The Polygon blockchain integration furnished an additional trust layer by committing fund allocation events as
immutable on-chain records. A principal architectural observation arising from the partial implementation was that
only high-value allocation data warranted on-chain storage, while comprehensive donor profile information was
retained off-chain within the relational database. This hybrid strategy effectively balances the transparency and
privacy imperatives while preserving system efficiency. The resulting transaction hash references facilitate
independently verifiable tracking without exposing personally identifiable information—a non-negotiable
requirement in operational philanthropic systems.
From a functional standpoint, the allocation mechanism demonstrated an unambiguous progression from donation
pooling through to beneficiary fund distribution. Partial analysis of allocation records confirmed that the
proportional contribution logic can reliably map donor funds to specific beneficiary outcomes. This capability
materially enhances donor trust by enabling concrete impact quantification rather than reliance on abstract
organizational reporting.
Certain limitations were surfaced during the partial analysis phase. Blockchain transaction latency, while
acceptable on the Polygon test network under evaluation conditions, may fluctuate under real-world production
loads and could periodically affect the immediacy of donor dashboard updates. Furthermore, while the current
implementation delivers foundational transparency capabilities, advanced analytical functions—such as predictive
allocation modelling or automated anomaly flagging—remain outside the current implementation scope.
On balance, the system achieves its stated primary objective of enhancing donation transparency while sustaining
efficient backend operations. The coupling of relational database management with blockchain-anchored
auditability establishes a scalable architectural foundation amenable to extension with AI-driven decision support
and advanced monitoring infrastructure in subsequent development cycles.
5.1 Future Enhancements
Several targeted enhancements are identified as priorities for strengthening the platform's capabilities in future
iterations:
– AI-Optimized Expenditure Forecasting: Applying machine learning models to historical donor
behaviour patterns to derive spending strategies that maximize aggregate donor satisfaction and
demonstrable social impact.
– Personalized Cause Recommendations: Enabling dynamically generated cause suggestions calibrated
to individual donor contribution histories and declared preferences.
– Multi-Cause Donation Distribution: Allowing donors to apportion a single fixed contribution across
multiple cause pools while preserving independent proportional tracking per pool.
– Third-Party Audit Integration: Incorporating external auditor workflows so that independent verifiers
can cryptographically validate expenditure records prior to on-chain anchoring, enhancing institutional
credibility.
– Beneficiary Feedback Integration: Linking impact reports submitted directly by recipients to
corresponding expenditure records, thereby closing the transparency loop between donors, platform
administrators, and beneficiaries.

6 Conclusion

This work confronts a foundational challenge within the global philanthropic sector: the persistent absence of
verifiable accountability and meaningful transparency in conventional donation management architectures. As
donors increasingly demand clear, real-time visibility into how their contributions are utilized, incumbent
centralized infrastructures remain structurally ill-equipped to deliver such assurances. This study establishes that
distributed ledger technology, deployed in conjunction with a mathematically rigorous proportional fund
deduction mechanism, provides a viable and practically implementable pathway to bridging this institutional trust
deficit.
The central contribution of this research is the design and partial validation of a proportional deduction framework
in which cause pool expenditures automatically and transparently adjust each contributing donor's balance
according to their relative funding share. By recording these adjustments immutably on the blockchain and
propagating real-time notifications to affected donors, the platform transforms the donor experience from passive
trust-based acceptance to active, cryptographically verifiable impact visibility. This mechanism simultaneously
advances transparency, equity, and accountability without sacrificing operational efficiency.
The architectural design and preliminary implementation results affirm the technical feasibility of the proposed
approach. Nevertheless, substantive practical challenges persist, including transaction throughput optimization
under heavy load, user experience simplification for non-technical donor populations, and navigation of an
evolving regulatory landscape governing blockchain-based financial record systems. These open challenges define
a rich agenda for future interdisciplinary research and policy engagement.
Planned future work encompasses full smart contract deployment, enhanced notification and system monitoring
infrastructure, real-world pilot evaluations conducted in partnership with operational charitable organizations, and
the formulation of regulatory-aligned hybrid record-keeping frameworks. In aggregate, Viyom embodies progress
toward mathematically verifiable philanthropy—a paradigm in which transparency is enforced through
cryptographic mechanisms rather than institutional reputation, contributing to the construction of accountable,
scalable, and technically robust digital donation ecosystems capable of sustaining long-term donor confidence and
delivering measurable societal impact.
